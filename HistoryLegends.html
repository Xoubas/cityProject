History and legends

Javier Bayón Quintas

Last updated on October 14th, 2022


The history of Tokyo began not too long ago, that's because until around 400 years ago, the official capital of Japan was still Kyoto, Tokyo was still a populous
fishing village in the east named Edo. Then, when in 1603, Tokugawa Ieyasu, the current shogun decided to stablish the Tokugawa Shogunate in Edo, marking the begining
of the Edo period. The Edo period would bring 250 years of stability to Japan until 1868, when the Meiji Restoration and the Boshin War started, bringing back the
imperial system and putting an end to the shogunate.

When the Tokugawa Shogunate was overthrown, the imperial capital became Edo, and its name was officially changed to Tokyo. During the Meiji era (1868-1912), Japan embraced
a large modernization process, with a huge influence from Western culture. The introduction of many foreign technologies into Japan, allowed for a fast modernization and economic
growth of the region. The outlawing of some traditional practices was implemented among society, some good, like the revokal of the burakumin
status during the Blood tax riots; and some not so admirable, like the forceful cutting of the chonmage hairstyle, seen a symbol of pride and honor by many.

After the Meiji era came the Taisho era, mostly known for the improvements of educational standards and the increasing numbre of women who decided to get higher
level education. In 1923 Tokyo was struck by the Great Kanto Earthquake, that devastated around 300.000 homes and affected 140.000 people.

From 1926 to 1989 the Showa era took place. Many important events occurred during this period, possibly the most well known might be World War II. Although a lot of technological
advances were made too. In 1927 the first subway line was inaugurated beteween Ueno and Asakusa, universal male suffrage was approved, Tokyo Airport was opened in 1931... And all
this before the begining of WW2. However, the Second World War took a heavy toll on Japan and the city of Tokyo was devastated during the 1945 bombings.

After the events of the war, Japan started an extensive recovery plan to overcome the difficult situation of the country. After Japan joined the United Nations in 1956, a great of
great economic growth started in the country leading to the 1964 Tokyo Olimpics, the inauguration of the Bullet Train, and many other achievements to come.


Legends:

The city of Tokyo (previously Edo), has been the stage of many important Japanese historical events that date centuries back. Due to that, and to the rich religious and traditional heritance
from Shintoism and Japanese culture, many miths and legends have arised through the years, of which Tokyo is the protagonist of some.


1. Sengaku-Ji

The tale of the 47 Ronin, is one of the oldest and most known stories in Japan's history. It is said that a group of samurai lost their master, who was forced to commit seppuku after attacking
the master of ceremonies Kira Yoshinaka. The 47 samurai became what's known as a ronin, after they lost their master, and decided to take revenge in behalf of his old master. After one year of
dissapearance, they organized to attack Yoshinaka's castle and kill him. After their success, staying true to their code, they turned themselves in to the authorities and were allowed an honorable
death committing seppuku.

The story, based on real life events, is set as an example of the Bushido honor code and loyalty of the former samurai to their master. They are all buried in the Buddish temple Sengaku-Ji,
in Tokyo.


2. Obake Kaidan

A famous set of stairs named "the ghost stairs", located in the Nezu district. This infamous stairs are known for possessing the unique feature of having forty steps on the way up but only
having 39 on the way down. It is said that the steps are made out of gravestones, having a gloomy mysterious athmosphere around them.


3. Gotokuji Temple

The story surrounding the temple is based on a previous priest's cat, who guided a lord and their men to safety under a dangerous storm. When the storm passed by, the lord as a tokken of gratitude offered land, rice and choose the
temple as the resting place for he and his family.

Nowadays the temple is filled with all sorts of cat figures with a raised paw, surrounding the goddess of mercy, Kannon, symbolizing the cat waving at the lords party.
