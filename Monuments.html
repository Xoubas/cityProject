Tokyo's monuments

Tokyo is the capital of Japan, and the most populated city in the world. It is filled with breathtaking highscrapers and the latest advances in technology,
but also has a more traditional side to it, which can be reflected by it's shintoist shrines and temples.

Although Tokyo is a large metropolis with around 40 million people living in it, and it's usually related to narrow streets crowded with people, while being
flooded by neon lights, it also has a great deal of beatiful parks, that definetily stand out in one of the busiest's cities in the world.

1. Meiji Jingu Shrine:

It is one of the most sacred shrines in Japan and it was build to honor the spirits of the former Emperor Meiji and his wife.

It's surrounded by a hundred acres forest in the middle of Tokyo. The forest is formed by around a hundred thousand trees from every prefecture of Japan.

Many important festivities are celebrated every year on its grounds, but the most important are the ring-entering ceremony performed by a Yokozuna,
called Dezuiri (手数入り), during the month of January. The Autumn Grand Festival is also celebrated from the 31st of October, to November the 3rd,
on the Emperor's birthday.

Other festivals:

  • Oharae (Great purification)
  • Joyasai (Year-end ritual)
  • Kigensai (National Foundation Day Festival)
  • Shoken-Kotaigo-Sai (Empress Shoken Memorial Ceremony)

2. Ueno Park:

It is one of the must see locations in Japan, situated in the neighbourhood of Taitō-ku. It was constructed in the location of the temple of Kan'ei-ji
which was destroyed in the Boshin War, and of which only remains one of its pagodas.

There are a lot of tourist attractions such as two museums, shrines, a small pond where people can ride paddle boats, a statue of the samurai Saigo Takamori with his
dog and even a zoo.

A very famous Japanese tradition is celebrated here, called Hanami (Cherry Viewing Party), that is centered on the cherry trees blossom and mainly consists of
an outdoor party with friends and family under a sakura.

3. Hachiko Memorial Statue:

Hachiko was a real dog who used to wait for his owner to return from work every day in front of Shibuya Railway Station.

Because of his dedication to his owner an statue was erected in its honour in 1934, becoming a famous landmark of the city. Every march there's a special ceremony held to
honour the faithful dog.

Hachiko and his owner are buried together in the Aoyama Cemetery, where he will keep accompanying his owner forever.
